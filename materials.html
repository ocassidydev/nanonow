Materials

Dots, lines, and sheets

Nanomaterials can be defined as any solid with a nanoscopic dimension. By dimension, we don't mean some kind of crazy alternate reality, just the somewhat less exciting definition of a measurement of length.

In the nomenclature of nanoscience, it is typical to define different nanomaterial categories with respect to the number of non-nanoscopic (>100nm) dimensions they posess. Nanoscopic dimensions are treated as effectively non-existence due to their incredibly small size. As such materials are referred to as being lower dimensions than the 3-D objects we are used to - either dots lines or sheets! Read on to learn more.

The dots: 0-D materials

We'll begin by looking at the simplest of all nanomaterials: those with only nanoscopic dimensions, the 0-D materials. These are structures, mostly (but not necessarily) spheres that can be as few as a dozen atoms wide. Broadly speaking, two types of these exist: metallic nanoparticles, and extended molecular structures.

Metallic nanoparticles are self-descriptive - extremely small bits of metal. This seems unremarkable at first, until acknowledging a simple fact - at such a small volume, much of the constituent atoms of these particles are at or close to the surface. This gives rise to vastly different behavior than is observed with "bulk" metal, as being dominated by the surface atoms causes very different chemistry - namely a greater level of reactivity. As such, they are often used as catalysts.

The famous example of extended molecular structures are the fullerenes, namely buckministerfullerene (C60) affectionately named after Buckminster Fuller for their geodesic dome shape, pictured above and in the figure. These are spherical molecules of carbon bonded to itself, and were first dicovered accidentally in soot in 1985. Buckministerfullerene is a good benchmark the nanoscale as it has a diameter of 1.1 nm.

Other extended molecular structures are also possible, such as nanoparticles made out of lipid molecules, which have gained interest in recent years as a method of drug delivery, notably used in many of the COVID-19 vaccines that were offered. Here of course the definition starts to get fuzzy - if any extended structure on the under 100 nm can be called 0-D, then many viruses and hemoglobin count!

Metallic nanoparticles: there are as many types of these as there are metals, and they can come in many shapes other than spheres.

Buckministerfullerene: these cages of carbon are like molecular footballs. They are also referred to as "Buckieballs."

A lipid nanoparticle, or liposome. Biologically active agents, such as RNA, can be contained within.

The lines: 1-D materials

Next we move onto somewhat more stringy class of nanomaterial: those with 1 non-nanoscopic dimension, the 1-D materials. These types of structures are classed by having a length at least 1000 times greater than their width and thickness, hence being described as lines and 1-dimensional. Two examples we'll discuss are nanotubes and nanowires.

Nanotubes are extended linear molecular structures. The most famous of these are single-walled carbon nanotubes (SWCNTs) which are pictured. As these structures have walls built out of molecular bonds, they are extremely tough and hard to snap mechanically. This gives nanotubes enourmous tensile strength, with SWCNTs being reported as having tensile strengths in the range of 13-53 GPa - roughly between 10 and 50 times tougher than stainless steel!

An interesting caveat about nanotubes is the fact that their properties change depending on their configuration - how the individual atoms are arranged with respect to each other in to make the tube walls. As an example, some conformations of SWCNTs can be conductive, while others are semiconducting.

As well as nanotubes, we have nanowires. What metallic nanoparticles are to 0-D materials, nanowires are to 1-D materials - extremely thin strands of metal. Similar to nanoparticles, the enhanced surfaced area causes different behaviour compared to their bulk counterparts. One potential use of these is as waveguides for light to enable photonics - which would mean information processing devices that use light instead of electricity!

It is also interesting to note something familiar to us counts as a 1-D material: DNA. This is in effect a linear crystal that can be up to a few metres long, while only being a few nanometres wide.

Carbon nanotubes: these strands of carbon have super strength!

Nanowires: these materials can act as wires for light.

DNA: the most advanced piece of nanotechnology we know of.

The sheets: 2-D Materials

Finally, we come to perhaps the most widely-studied class of nanomaterial: those with 2 non-nanoscopic dimensions, the 2-D materials. These structures are classified by having lengths and widths at least 1000 times greater than their thickness.
