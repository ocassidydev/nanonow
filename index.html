The frontier of material science

Why nano?

Nanomaterials have incredible properties which will have outsized impact in our everyday, from the clothes we wear, to the devices we use, to the medicines we take. Already, many commercially-available products rely on nanotech.

Knowledge of the science behind these materials is key to multi-disciplinary innovation in this frontier, along with raising public awareness of the enormous potential of nano.

As you will soon learn, there are massive gulfs of space between the the scale of our everyday and the nanoscale. This is what originally evoked such interest in nanotechnology, best captured by a famous quote from celebrated physicist Richard Feynmann: "There's plenty of room at the bottom."

Whether you have no scientific background or are an expert in another field, this site is for you. Read on to learn more!

Some potential applications of nanomaterials, clockwise from top left: ultratough yet light body armour, light-absorbing and self-cleaning coatings for solar panels, material enabling hydrogen storage and fuel cell technology, liquid-repelling textiles that never stain.

How small is nano?

Nanomaterials are materials which have features which can be measured in nanometers, a unit of length. To get a sense of how small this is, we'll shrink down from the sizes we're used to, and benchmark against familiar objects.

Consider 1 meter. This is a familiar unit of measurement. The average human stands between 1.5 to 2 metres tall. Down from this is 1 centimeter, or 1 hundreth of a metre. Typical coins have diameters between 2 and 5 centimeters. Collectively, we refer to things on this scale as "the macroscale", macro meaning "big".

Further down from that, is a millimetre, 1 thousandth of a metre. Typical pin heads are about 1 millimetre in diameter, and we can call such objects milliscaled.

Now we'll go even smaller. At 1000 times smaller than a millimetre, we hit a micrometre. A good example of a microscaled object are strands of human hair, which typically have widths between 20 and 200 micrometres. Given the expression "a hairs breadth away", you can imagine how small just one micrometre is!

We still aren't there yet though. 1000 times smaller than a micrometre, is where we're finally at a nanometre, or 1 billionth of a metre. At the nanoscale, individual atoms become significant in size.

It is the fact that nanomaterials have near-atomic resolution that causes their amazing properties. Check out the materials section to learn more!
