Why nano?

Nanomaterials have incredible properties which will have outsized impact in our everyday, from the clothes we wear, to the devices we use, to the medicines we take. Already, many commercially-available products rely on nanotech.

Knowledge of the science behind these materials is key to multi-disciplinary innovation in this frontier, along with raising public awareness of the enormous potential of nano.

As you will soon learn, there are massive gulfs of space between the the scale of our everyday and the nanoscale. This is what originally evoked such interest in nanotechnology, best captured by a famous remark from celebrated physicist Richard Feynmann: "There's plenty of room at the bottom."

Whether you have no scientific background or are an expert in another field, this site is for you. Read on to learn more!

Some potential applications of nanomaterials, clockwise from top left: ultratough yet light body armour, light-absorbing and self-cleaning coatings for solar panels, material enabling hydrogen storage and fuel cell technology, liquid-repelling textiles that never stain.

Just how small is nano?

Nanomaterials are materials which have features which can be measured in nanometers, a unit of length. To get a sense of how small this is, we'll shrink down from the sizes we're used to, and benchmark against familiar objects.

Consider 1 meter. This is a familiar unit of measurement, for which we use the symbol m. The average human stands between 1.5 to 2 m tall.

Down from this, we come to 1 centimeter, or 1 hundreth of a metre, which has the symbol cm. Typical coins have diameters between 2 and 5 cm. Collectively, we refer to anything at or above this scale as "the macroscale", macro meaning "big". Although it may seem counterintuitive that a coin could be considered big - compared to how small things can be, it's huge!

Further down from that, we get to millimetre, 1 thousandth of a metre, symbolized by mm. Typical pin heads are about 1 mm in diameter, and we call such objects milliscaled.

Now we'll go even smaller. At 1000 times smaller than a millimetre, we hit a micrometre, symbol μm. A good example of a microscaled object are strands of human hair, which typically have widths between 20 and 200 micrometres. Given the expression "a hair's breadth away", one can imagine how small just one micrometre is!

We still aren't there yet though. 1000 times smaller than a micrometre, is where we finally arrive at a nanometre, symbol nm. This is 1 billionth of a metre. At the nanoscale, individual atoms and molecules become significant in size.

It is the fact that nanomaterials have near-atomic resolution that causes their amazing properties. Check out the materials section to learn more!
